# Learning methods — one-page summaries (sources named, wording our own)

The skill's method library names these; this page is the slightly longer
reference. None of it is secret sauce — the value is applying it through the
learner's *own material and ledger*, with honest grading.

## Practice testing & distributed practice — the two with the strongest evidence

Dunlosky, Rawson, Marsh, Nathan & Willingham (2013), *Improving Students'
Learning With Effective Learning Techniques* (Psychological Science in the
Public Interest, 14(1)) reviewed ten common techniques. Only two earned a
**high-utility** rating across ages, materials and testing conditions:

| Technique | What it means in a session |
|---|---|
| **Practice testing** (active recall) | Retrieve from memory *before* seeing the answer — being asked beats being shown |
| **Distributed practice** (spacing) | Several short returns to the material beat one long cram, for anything you want to keep |

Rereading, highlighting and summarization — the habits most learners default
to — rated low. That asymmetry is this skill's whole reason to exist.

## Spaced repetition and the SM-2 lineage

Ebbinghaus's forgetting curve (1885): retention decays fast, and each
successful recall flattens the curve. SM-2 (Piotr Woźniak, 1987, the
SuperMemo-2 algorithm — the ancestor of Anki-style scheduling) operationalizes
this: each item carries an ease factor (initial 2.5, floor 1.3), and the
interval to the next review grows by that factor after each successful recall,
shrinking back when recall fails.

This skill uses the **idea**, not the arithmetic: a default ladder of
**1 → 3 → 7 → 14 → 30 days**, where the learner's own rating
(again / hard / good / easy) moves an item down or up the ladder. The honest
version of "the algorithm" for a personal ledger is: *reviews get further
apart while recall holds, and a miss brings the item back soon.* Anything
fancier belongs in a dedicated SRS app, and pretending otherwise would be
false precision.

## Feynman technique — teach it to find the gaps

Named for Richard Feynman's habit of testing understanding by explanation:
pick a concept, explain it in plain words as if to someone new, notice
exactly where you stall or reach for jargon, go back to the source for that
piece, repeat. In a session the buddy's role is the *listener*: prompt,
probe the vague spots ("what does that word do in your sentence?"), and
resist explaining it for them — the learner's stall is the data.

## Cornell notes (Walter Pauk, *How to Study in College*)

Page split into three zones: a narrow **cue column** (questions/keywords,
written after class), the main **notes** area, and a one-or-two-line
**summary** at the bottom. Two properties matter here: writing cues and a
summary forces one retrieval pass over fresh notes, and the cue column is a
ready-made quiz bank — cover the notes, answer from the cues. Offer the format
when a learner's notes are an undifferentiated wall of text; never demand it.

## Bloom's taxonomy — a ladder for question depth

The revised taxonomy (Anderson & Krathwohl 2001): **remember → understand →
apply → analyze → evaluate → create**. Used here as a quiz-writing ladder:

| Rung | Question shape |
|---|---|
| remember | "What is X?" |
| understand | "Explain X in your own words" (Feynman turf) |
| apply | "Use X to solve this new case" |
| analyze | "How do X and Y differ, and why does it matter?" |
| evaluate | "Which approach is better here, and on what grounds?" |
| create | "Design something that needs X" |

A learner who aces every "remember" question may still fail "apply" — staying
on the bottom rung because it feels good is the quiz-shaped version of
rereading. Climb one rung when the current one holds; say you're doing it.

## What this skill deliberately does not claim

No mastery percentages (the ledger's real intervals and mistake history are
the progress report); no neuroscience folklore ("learning styles" matching has
no solid evidence base — see the same Dunlosky-adjacent literature); no
automatic timers (reminders are scheduled by the user, or by Karvy at their
request — never silently by a study session).
